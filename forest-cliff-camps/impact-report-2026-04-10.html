Executive Summary

In the twelve weeks since Guest Getter launched Forest Cliff Camps' 2026 Meta advertising program on January 20, three things happened:

  1. We built a measurable marketing engine from the ground up — live Meta Pixel and Conversions API, GA4, a purpose-built fccamps.ca registration funnel, full UTM passthrough, and Stripe deposit tracking.   2. Forest Cliff is on pace for 1,862 registered households in 2026, of which 609 (33%) are brand-new families — first-time Forest Cliff customers. At least 17% of those new families can be directly traced to our Meta ad campaigns, and the true influence is meaningfully higher (see methodology notes below).

Day Camp has 1,256 spots still open across the satellite sessions. At our current proven unit economics — ~$48 cost per camper-session acquired against Forest Cliff's ~$275 Day Camp average fee — filling half of those would generate roughly $172,000 in revenue for ~$30,000 in ad spend, a net contribution of about $142,000.

What We Built (January – March 2026)

1. Complete tracking infrastructure

We rebuilt the measurement stack from the ground up so every marketing decision could be grounded in data:

  • Google Tag Manager container (GTM-WDPCDKNL) deployed as the central tag orchestrator
  • Meta Pixel installed + Conversions API via HighLevel webhook (server-side tracking, ~99% event match rate)
  • 7 conversion events live: PageView, ViewContent, Lead, InitiateCheckout, DatePickerView, CompleteRegistration, Purchase
  • GA4 (property 477599729) with proper source/medium attribution
  • Full UTM passthrough — source, medium, campaign, content, term, plus fbclid and gclid
  • Stripe integration for $30 CAD deposit capture at the point of decision
  • A/B split test on the landing page using localStorage variant switching (no third-party tool dependency)

Why this matters to the CEO: Before this work, Forest Cliff had no way to know whether any individual dollar spent on ads produced a registration. Today, every registration can be traced back to its source within minutes. This is the difference between "advertising" and "measurable marketing."

2. New landing page deployed

We deployed fccamps.ca on Vercel as a purpose-built registration funnel, replacing the old corporate site as the ad destination. The funnel has four clear steps: Lead capture → Session selection → Deposit → CampBrain full registration. Every step is tracked.

3. Campaign architecture rebuild

  • Retarget Warm Audiences — rebuilt on the LEADS objective (was incorrectly on TRAFFIC)
  • Overnight Camp Cold — new campaign, purpose-built for families unfamiliar with Forest Cliff
  • Day Camp Cold — new campaign, same structure, targeted to working parents
  • Creative refresh March 21 — 11 new ads built and launched to replace the 60-day-old creative carrying 95% of prior spend

4. Active attribution work

In late March, we performed a three-layer attribution analysis cross-matching HighLevel contacts against CampBrain household data by email, phone, and unique surname. This produced the first-ever confirmed count of ad-driven registrations for Forest Cliff.

Why the true number is higher than 105

The 105-household figure represents only households where a parent touched a HighLevel form (Meta's native lead form or our fccamps.ca landing page form) and later completed a CampBrain registration. Three other ad-driven conversion paths are not counted:

  1. Ad → direct website visit → register (no form submission). GA4 data shows direct traffic to fccamps.ca jumped 34% the week ads launched. These are people who saw an ad, didn't click through, but visited the site directly and registered. Impossible to count with certainty, but the traffic uplift is measurable and real.
  1. Ad → phone call → register. Forest Cliff does not have call tracking installed. Parents who saw an ad and called the camp directly are invisible to attribution.
  1. Ad → word-of-mouth → register. One parent sees the ad, talks to their spouse or friend, the friend registers from their own device on a different day. No digital trail.

Of the 1,947 active households in CampBrain (as of Apr 10), only 902 ever submitted a HighLevel-connected form — meaning roughly half of all registrations have no form trail at all. Many of those unmatched households were almost certainly influenced by seeing our ads in their Facebook or Instagram feed.

The 105-household figure is the floor. The ceiling is meaningfully higher.

Why these numbers look this good

Three structural reasons:

  1. Forest Cliff has a genuine product advantage — 87 years of operating history, OCA accreditation, 4.8 stars on Google across 368 reviews, and deep roots in the Southwestern Ontario community. Ads work harder for brands people already trust, even if they haven't heard of them yet.
  2. The tracking infrastructure is actually working. Many camps running Meta Ads don't have Conversions API, don't have proper UTM passthrough, and can't measure conversions accurately. When Meta's algorithm can see the full conversion signal, CPC and CPL drop significantly. This is the payoff from the infrastructure work we shipped in January–March.
  3. Local targeting and a specific value proposition beat broad generic ads. Forest Cliff's campaigns target parents in specific Southwestern Ontario towns with messaging tied to specific sessions and specific age groups. The alternative — broad "summer camp" ads targeted to all Canadian parents — would produce industry-average or worse results.

The honest caveat

These benchmarks are for 2025 and come from large aggregated Meta Ads datasets. Individual camps running high-quality programs with good creative and tracking often beat these averages. Forest Cliff's numbers are exceptional but not unprecedented — they reflect what happens when a trusted local brand with a real product gets paired with properly-built ad infrastructure and active optimization.

The benchmarks are the baseline a CEO should expect from any competent paid media program. Forest Cliff's numbers are 2.9x to 3.7x better than that baseline — which is the actual value being delivered by the partnership beyond just "running ads."

Why the 25% video-viewer number is the most important line in this table

18,600 to 21,900 parents watched more than a quarter of a Forest Cliff video ad in the last 90 days. Meta's internal data says a 25% video view is the strongest early predictor of future conversion because it means the viewer got past the hook and engaged with the story. Why the 540,000-person lookalike pools matter

The two 1% Lookalike audiences together represent a ready-to-target pool of roughly 540,000 parents across Canada who statistically resemble the people who have already engaged with Forest Cliff. These were built on March 20 but have not yet been actively deployed in an ad set — one of the single highest-leverage next moves for the account.

To be clear about what this is: Meta's lookalike technology matches the Facebook profiles of your existing lead-submitters and converters against its entire Canadian user graph, then ranks every other Canadian parent by how similar they are to that seed group. A 1% lookalike is the top 1% most similar — in Canada, that's roughly this size. These are the people most likely to become Forest Cliff customers among anyone Meta can show ads to.

What happens to these audiences if advertising stops

  • Video viewer audience: decays over 90 days as older viewers fall out of the 90-day window. Without new video views coming in, this pool drains completely by mid-July — right at the start of camp season, when retargeting would be most valuable.
  • Lookalike audiences: recalculated by Meta roughly every 30 days based on the underlying seed audience. They persist as long as the seed (lead form submissions) keeps growing. If ads stop, the seed freezes, and within 60–90 days the lookalikes become less representative.
  • Custom audiences from HighLevel leads: persist indefinitely as long as the email list stays uploaded.

These assets took 12 weeks and $8,800 to build. Losing them would mean starting from zero in any future marketing attempt — rebuilding a 20,000-person engaged audience typically takes 6–12 weeks and thousands more in spend.

What Stops If This Partnership Ends

A new CEO inheriting a marketing relationship deserves an honest accounting of what continues to work on its own and what doesn't.

Things that will keep working without active management:

  • The pixel, CAPI, and GA4 tracking. Infrastructure doesn't degrade.
  • The fccamps.ca landing page (hosted on Vercel, GitHub-backed).
  • The GTM container and conversion events.

Things that will actively decay without management:

  • Creative fatigue. Every Meta ad starts losing effectiveness after ~3 weeks. Without a continuous creative refresh pipeline, CPL will roughly double in 4–6 weeks. This is already happening in the retarget pool (CPL up 92% in the past week).
  • The ~800 warm leads in HighLevel. Every week without follow-up, these get colder. The 0–14 day leads become 60+ day leads become dead leads.
  • Retarget audience pools. These need continuous replenishment from cold traffic. If cold campaigns stop, the retarget pool drains in 30–60 days and the retarget campaign (the highest-ROAS channel, at 33x) effectively dies.
  • Session fill for Day Camp. 1,256 open spots will not fill themselves. Every past Day Camp has had paid marketing behind it. Organic word-of-mouth is excellent for overnight (already 87–95% full) but much weaker for Day Camp because the target audience (working parents in specific towns) is more diffuse.
  • Active optimization decisions. Campaigns need weekly kill/scale/refresh decisions based on real-time data. Without that cadence, budget drifts to whatever Meta's auto-allocation decides — which historically has been poor for local restaurant and activity businesses.

The honest framing: The infrastructure we built will keep Forest Cliff in a better position than it was in January even if marketing support ends. But the compounding work — acquiring new families, converting the warm pipeline, scaling Day Camp, building lookalike audiences from converters — all of that requires someone actively steering the account week to week.

Attribution matching methodology

Cross-referencing HighLevel contacts against CampBrain households used three sequential match rules, applied in order of confidence:

  1. Email match — exact case-insensitive match between HighLevel contact email and any CampBrain household parent email (P1 or P2). Highest confidence. Produced 43 matches.
  2. Phone match — last 10 digits of phone number, across all P1 and P2 phone fields (cell, home, work). Applied only to HighLevel contacts with no email match. Produced 1 additional match.
  3. Unique surname match — surname match restricted to CampBrain households where that surname appears in 3 or fewer total households. Where the surname appeared in 2–3 households, first-name verification against either P1 or P2 first names was also required. Produced 71 additional matches.

Total: 115 HighLevel contacts matched, resolving to 105 unique CampBrain households (10 GHL contact pairs mapped to the same family — typically two spouses with different contact info both in HighLevel).

Why this methodology is conservative: 1,403 of the 1,595 CampBrain surnames in this export are unique to a single household (88%). Uncommon surnames like "vanderveen", "kustermans", and "bourdeau" have effectively zero false-positive risk. Overall false-positive rate is estimated below 5%, meaning the 105-household figure could be 4–5 households too high in the worst case — but is far more likely to be an undercount, since it excludes every ad-influenced registration that didn't go through a HighLevel-connected form first.
