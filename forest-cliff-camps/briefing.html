01 / THE PROGRAM AT A GLANCE

Twelve weeks of measurable, traceable growth

Forest Cliff's 2026 Meta advertising program launched on January 20, 2026. Over the twelve weeks since, it has operated as a fully-instrumented acquisition engine — every dollar of ad spend is traceable to a lead, every lead is traceable to a registration, and every registration is traceable to a specific campaign and audience.

$8,808 in ad spend has been directly traced to 105 new households (~150 new campers) registering through CampBrain, generating roughly $83,200 in Year-1 revenue at an average cost of $59 per camper acquired. That's a 9.4× return on ad spend in Year 1 alone — and because Forest Cliff's own data shows the average returning family attends for 3.56 years, the projected 5-year value of those 105 families is between $223,000 and $416,000.

Industry context
The 2025 Facebook Ads benchmark for the Education industry is roughly $28 per lead and an implied $280 per customer acquired. Forest Cliff is currently running at $8 per lead and $59 per camper — about 3.4× more efficient than the industry average.

Infrastructure in place

The following systems were built and deployed between January and March as the measurement and acquisition infrastructure for the paid advertising program. They're all live and operating:

  • Meta Pixel and Conversions API — server-side tracking via HighLevel webhook, capturing the ~30–40% of events that iOS 14.5+ breaks on client-side tracking alone.
  • `fccamps.ca` registration funnel — four-step purpose-built funnel (Lead → Session → Deposit → CampBrain) deployed on Vercel with full UTM passthrough and Stripe integration.
  • Three active campaigns — Retarget Warm Audiences, Overnight Camp Cold, and Day Camp Cold, all running on the LEADS objective with proper attribution.
  • GA4 and GTM (container `GTM-WDPCDKNL`, property 477599729) for second-layer analytics and source attribution.
  • Weekly performance monitoring — kill/scale/refresh decisions documented with data-backed reasoning.

03 / THE CURRENT PLAYBOOK

What's in motion and what needs attention

The paid advertising engine is running. What follows is an inventory of the specific initiatives currently in motion within this workstream, the assets that exist but haven't yet been activated, and the operational items that require ongoing attention to keep the program compounding.

Warm lead pipeline awaiting follow-up

High Leverage

Approximately 800 families have submitted a Meta lead form or direct `fccamps.ca` form but have not yet completed a CampBrain registration. They have email addresses, phone numbers, and in most cases interest tags (day camp vs. overnight, grade level). A structured follow-up sequence — automated SMS + email touches over 2–4 weeks — would likely convert a meaningful share of this pool at zero additional ad spend. This is the single largest piece of unshipped work in the account.

Lookalike audiences built, not yet deployed

Ready to Activate

Two 1% Lookalike audiences were built on March 20 — one seeded from lead events, one from lead conversions — together covering roughly 540,000 Canadian parents who statistically resemble families already converting. Neither has been activated in an ad set yet. Deploying them in the cold campaigns is a short, high-leverage task that should meaningfully improve cold acquisition efficiency.

Day Camp capacity is the open opportunity

$345K Unbooked

Of Forest Cliff's 1,256 remaining open spots, effectively all of them are in the Day Camp satellite programs (DC1–DC8, ACT add-ons, and a handful of Leadership / Launch seats). At the ~$48-per-camper-session acquisition cost demonstrated during the first twelve weeks, the economics of continuing to push Day Camp fill are favorable through the start of camp in early July.

Creative refresh cycle needs ongoing attention

Operational

Meta ads lose effectiveness after roughly three weeks as audiences saturate. The current creative library was refreshed on March 21 with 11 new ad variants and is beginning to show early fatigue signals in the retarget pool (cost per lead rising, click-through rate declining). A refresh of 3–5 new creatives every 2–3 weeks is the standard cadence to keep the program compounding. Without it, cost per lead can roughly double in 4–6 weeks.

Retarget audience pool needs continuous replenishment

Operational

The retarget campaign is currently the highest-ROAS channel in the account (historically 25–33× on a good week). Its effectiveness depends on a steady flow of new cold traffic to replenish the warm audience pool. The video-viewer audience decays over 90 days as older viewers fall out of the window — if cold traffic stops flowing, the retarget engine loses fuel within roughly 60–90 days. Keeping a baseline of cold campaign spend is what keeps retarget profitable.

Weekly monitoring cadence in place

Running

Between March 20 and April 10, seven formal performance reports were produced (Mar 23, 24, 27, 28, 30, Apr 1, Apr 10) — each documenting kill/scale/refresh decisions against Forest Cliff-specific thresholds with audience-layer comparison, frequency tracking, and prioritized action lists. All reports are archived and available for reference.

Attribution confidence and caveats

The 105-household figure comes from a three-layer sequential match between HighLevel contact data and CampBrain's household export, restricted to contacts whose source is a Meta lead form or the `fccamps.ca` landing page. It is deliberately conservative: the false-positive rate is estimated below 5%, and the figure excludes any ad-influenced registration that didn't go through a HighLevel-connected form (direct website visits, phone calls, word-of-mouth from ad-influenced families). The true count of ad-driven registrations is almost certainly higher than 105 — we simply can't prove the ones above that number with evidence a skeptic couldn't argue with.

GA4 data corroborates the undercounting: direct traffic to `fccamps.ca` jumped +34% the week ads launched, and that uplift has held for the full twelve-week window. Those are visitors who saw an ad, didn't click through, but navigated to the site directly — a known and real ad-driven path that doesn't leave a lead-form trail.
